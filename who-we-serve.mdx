ByteWise works exclusively with educational institutions — from preschools to universities. Education has technology needs that are fundamentally different from those of commercial organizations: schools manage sensitive data for minors, operate on constrained budgets, serve highly varied user populations, and must balance security with accessibility. A one-size-fits-all approach to IT and cybersecurity does not work in this context.

## The educational sector's technology context

Schools are increasingly dependent on technology for administration, communication, and instruction. Student records, financial systems, learning platforms, and communication tools are all digitally managed — often across multiple disconnected systems. Despite this dependence, most educational institutions do not have dedicated IT teams or in-house security expertise. Technology decisions are frequently made by administrators or teachers who lack the technical background to evaluate risks or plan infrastructure. The result is a sector that is digitally active but structurally underprepared.

## What schools typically lack

Most educational institutions share a common set of gaps, regardless of size or level.

<Steps>
  <Step title="No in-house IT security expertise">
    Schools rarely employ cybersecurity professionals. Technology is managed by generalists — or not managed at all — leaving critical vulnerabilities unaddressed.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Reactive rather than proactive security posture">
    Institutions typically respond to incidents after they occur. Without a structured security program, threats go undetected until they cause real damage.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Unstructured or outdated infrastructure">
    Networks, devices, and software are often acquired piecemeal over many years. The result is fragmented systems with no clear ownership, documentation, or update cycle.
  </Step>
  <Step title="No visibility into operational or digital risks">
    Most schools do not have a current inventory of their technology assets, let alone an understanding of which systems carry the greatest risk or operational dependency.
  </Step>
</Steps>
